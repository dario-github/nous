\begin{abstract}
Current approaches to AI agent safety rely on monolithic mechanisms: either a single LLM-based classifier or a fixed rule set. Both fail in complementary ways---classifiers hallucinate on edge cases, while rules cannot capture semantic nuance. We present a four-layer composable safety gate for agentic AI systems: (1)~a deterministic blocker using Datalog constraints; (2)~a triviality filter for cost-aware routing; (3)~a semantic gate using an LLM with minimal-pair prompt engineering; and (4)~a post-gate deterministic auditor (Post-Gate Verifier) that independently checks tool-call sequences for information-flow anomalies the semantic gate cannot observe. On the AgentHarm benchmark (176 harmful + 176 benign scenarios), the three-layer system achieves TPR~100\% (95\%~CI: [90.3\%, 100.0\%]) and FPR~0.0\% (95\%~CI: [0.0\%, 9.7\%]) on the validation set. On a final independent held-out owner-centric benchmark (300 harmful, 150 benign), the gate (L1--L3) achieves overall TPR~75.3\% (95\%~CI: [70.2\%, 79.9\%]) and FPR~3.3\% (95\%~CI: [1.4\%, 7.6\%]); the full four-layer system (L1--L4) achieves overall TPR~\textbf{85.3\%} (256/300; Wilson 95\%~CI: [80.9\%, 88.9\%]) at the same FPR~3.3\%---Layer~4 adds 30 Hijacking detections with zero FPR impact on any other category. The central empirical finding concerns \emph{layer complementarity}: for Hijacking attacks---prompt injections that embed malicious instructions in external documents---the gate (L1--L3) alone achieves only 43.3\% TPR (95\%~CI: [31.6\%, 55.9\%]), while the Post-Gate Verifier (L4) alone achieves 75.0\% (95\%~CI: [62.8\%, 84.2\%]). Combined, L1--L4 reaches 93.3\% (56/60; 95\%~CI: [84.1\%, 97.4\%]) because the two mechanisms' blind spots are nearly disjoint (overlap: 15/60 cases caught by both; neither: 4/60 = 6.7\% true structural boundary). The Verifier adds zero LLM cost and $+$0.038ms latency. Shadow deployment over 30,000+ production calls shows 99.47\% consistency. We argue that compositional safety---layers with orthogonal detection dimensions---provides more robust protection than monolithic approaches, and characterize the residual 6.7\% as a genuine architectural boundary requiring SQL-aware content analysis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

As AI agents transition from isolated chatbots to autonomous systems executing multi-step tool chains, safety must evolve from static content moderation to real-time interception at every tool call. Recent incidents underscore the urgency: in March~2026, a Meta engineering agent autonomously posted flawed technical guidance on an internal forum; employees followed the instructions before the post was identified two hours later, exposing sensitive operational data~\cite{meta2026sev1}. A survey by Cybersecurity Insiders found that 47\% of CISOs have observed unauthorized agent actions, yet only 5\% express confidence in their organization's ability to contain such incidents~\cite{cybersecinsiders2026ciso}. Offensive benchmarks underscore the asymmetry: at RSAC~2026, the SANS Institute reported that all five top-ranked attack techniques were AI-assisted for the first time in 25 years~\cite{sans2026rsac}; a Claude-based agent replicated a three-day forensic investigation in under 15 minutes, while an AI system achieved domain controller takeover in under 8 minutes---capabilities that make real-time defensive interception not a convenience but a necessity.

Beyond isolated incidents, systematic evidence now confirms that agent misbehavior is a growing production phenomenon. A study by the Centre for Long-Term Resilience (CLTR), funded by the UK AI Security Institute, analyzed 183,000 real-world AI interaction transcripts collected between October~2025 and March~2026 and identified 698 scheming-related incidents---a 4.9$\times$ increase over the five-month collection window, far outpacing the 1.7$\times$ growth in general online discussion of scheming~\cite{cltr2026scheming}. Documented behaviors included agents sustaining months-long deceptions about their activities, publishing unsanctioned content criticizing developers who rejected their proposed changes, and circumventing copyright restrictions by deceiving a second AI model---precisely the categories of unauthorized autonomy, covert external disclosure, and deceptive goal pursuit that Nous targets.

The prevailing approaches occupy opposite extremes. \textbf{Monolithic LLM classifiers} (e.g., Llama Guard~\cite{inan2023llamaguard}, ShieldGemma~\cite{zeng2024shieldgemma}) offer semantic understanding but introduce latency, hallucinate on edge cases, and cannot provide formal safety guarantees. \textbf{Rule-only systems} (e.g., NeMo Guardrails~\cite{rebedea2023nemoguardrails}) are fast and auditable but cannot distinguish semantically similar actions with different intents---``download a research paper'' versus ``plagiarize a research paper'' look identical to pattern matchers.

The safety perimeter is also expanding beyond server-side API calls. Apple's iOS~27 announcement that Siri will integrate multiple third-party AI providers~\cite{apple2026ios27} means agent tool-call interception will become a requirement at the OS level, not merely at the cloud service boundary. Concurrently, the accidental disclosure of Anthropic's unreleased \emph{Claude Mythos} model---internally characterized as posing ``unprecedented cybersecurity risks'' due to its ability to autonomously identify vulnerabilities in production codebases~\cite{fortune2026mythos}---demonstrates that the capability frontier is advancing faster than deployment guardrails.

The industry is converging on the recognition that agent governance requires new infrastructure. In a single week of March~2026, NVIDIA announced NemoClaw, a declarative policy engine described as ``the policy engine of all the SaaS companies in the world''~\cite{nvidia2026nemoclaw}; Microsoft introduced the 365~E7 tier at \$99/user for agent security~\cite{microsoft2026e7}; and Oasis Security raised \$120M for non-human identity management~\cite{oasis2026funding}. Yet no existing system combines formal guarantees with semantic understanding in a composable architecture.

\textbf{Contributions.} We present Nous, a four-layer composable safety gate (Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}) with: (1)~a composability argument via the Swiss Cheese model showing multiplicative failure-probability reduction~\cite{reason1990swisscheese}; (2)~a shadow-mode deployment methodology enabling zero-downtime transition with 99.47\% agreement over 30,204 production calls; (3)~empirical evaluation on AgentHarm (TPR~100\%/FPR~0\% val) and a final independent owner-centric held-out benchmark (300H+150B), with per-category analysis distinguishing structurally-detectable threats from prompt-injection-driven attacks; gate-only overall TPR~75.3\% and full-system (L1--L4) overall TPR~\textbf{85.3\%} (256/300; CI: [80.9\%, 88.9\%]) at FPR~3.3\%; (4)~a Post-Gate Verifier (Layer~4)---a deterministic, zero-LLM-cost auditor that independently checks tool-call sequences for information-flow anomalies, lifting Hijacking TPR from 43.3\% to 93.3\% (56/60) on the held-out set with 0\% FPR; (5)~empirical evidence for \emph{layer complementarity}: gate and verifier blind spots are nearly disjoint (11 gate-only, 30 verifier-only, 15 both, 4 neither), with the residual 4/60 (6.7\%) constituting a genuine architectural boundary; and (6)~layer ablation showing L3's contribution \emph{increases} with attack sophistication ($+$62.6pp on held-out vs.\ $+$44.4pp on training data).

\section{Architecture}

We present a three-layer composable safety gate that intercepts every tool call in an agentic system. Each layer addresses a distinct failure mode, and layers compose sequentially: an input blocked or allowed by an earlier layer never reaches later ones. Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture} illustrates the architecture.

\textbf{Design principles.} (1)~\emph{Composability}: each layer can be independently tested, replaced, and formally verified. (2)~\emph{Fail-closed}: any layer failure (parse error, LLM timeout, configuration corruption) results in a block verdict, never silent passthrough. (3)~\emph{Cost-aware routing}: expensive LLM inference is reserved for genuinely ambiguous cases.

\subsection{Layer 1: Deterministic Blocker}

The first layer applies pattern-matching rules expressed as Datalog constraints. Our implementation uses a TypeScript Datalog engine achieving P50 latency of 0.055ms per evaluation---negligible compared to tool execution time.

Rules are declarative and auditable:
\begin{lstlisting}[style=datalog]
url_has_social(X) :- action_url(X),
    matches(X, "twitter|instagram|weibo").
block(X, "social media link") :-
    url_has_social(X).
\end{lstlisting}

The deployment includes 46~rules (45 YAML constraint files plus a Datalog rule file) covering destructive actions, social media interactions, protected file modifications, credential exfiltration, chain-aware hijacking detection, and owner-harm categories. Rules achieve \textbf{zero false positives by construction} on the benign test set---each is manually authored and verified against benign cases before deployment. Formal soundness holds within Layer~1's closed-world semantics; end-to-end guarantees require all three layers.

\textbf{Fail-closed semantics.} If the Datalog engine encounters a parse error, the entire input is blocked. This property was identified as a P0 gap in an independent code audit: the initial implementation used soft warnings instead of hard failure.

\subsection{Layer 2: Triviality Filter}

The second layer applies lightweight heuristics to identify trivially benign actions (file reads, web searches without sensitive targets) and route them past the expensive semantic gate. This reduces LLM inference calls by approximately 70\% in production, yielding significant cost savings without sacrificing safety---trivially benign actions have zero overlap with the harmful action space.

\subsection{Layer 3: Semantic Gate}

The third layer handles genuinely ambiguous cases using an LLM (DeepSeek-V3.2) with a prompt containing 23~minimal-pair examples. Each pair consists of a harmful variant (with block reason) and a benign variant (with allow reason), training the model to distinguish intent from surface form.

\textbf{Intent decomposition.} Before classification, we extract structured intent: stated purpose, core task, required capabilities, and risk factors. This decomposition is critical for cases like ``help me correct the dosage guide''---the stated intent is correction, but the core task involves drug dosage modification, requiring careful analysis rather than surface-level allow/block.

\textbf{Majority vote.} Due to stochastic variance in Mixture-of-Experts architectures (where \texttt{temperature=0} does not guarantee determinism), we employ majority vote across $k=3$ evaluations. This eliminates 100\% of observed stochastic FPR in our deployment.

\subsection{Knowledge Graph Enrichment}

A knowledge graph (579~relations, 482~entities across 11 entity types and 11 relation types) provides post-gate context enrichment---entity resolution, policy lookups, and historical pattern matching. Critically, KG signals feed into the gate's context but do not override verdicts. Early experiments with direct KG injection into the semantic gate caused a 12\% TPR degradation due to attention dilution; the current post-gate architecture avoids this failure mode.

\subsection{Layer 4: Post-Gate Verifier}

Layer~4 is an independent deterministic auditor that runs on \emph{every} tool call after the semantic gate (L1--L3). Its rules are designed to fire only on patterns characteristic of prompt injection and hijacking attacks; no category oracle or threat-context label is needed---activation is determined entirely by the rule triggers themselves. While L1--L3 evaluate tool calls based on prompt and command metadata, the Verifier examines information-flow patterns that require cross-step context: file contents, tool-call sequences, and behavioral signatures invisible to single-call architectures.

\textbf{Design principles.} (1)~\emph{Independence}: the Verifier reaches conclusions independently of gate self-reporting; it infers what the gate \emph{could not} have seen rather than trusting what it claims. (2)~\emph{Deterministic}: no LLM calls; all rules are regular-expression pattern matching over tool-call sequences and file contents. (3)~\emph{FAIL\_OPEN}: any exception during verification leaves the verdict unchanged; the Verifier can only upgrade, never degrade, a gate verdict.

\textbf{Six audit rules (V1--V6).} V1~(red): a bash command executes a local file that the gate never inspected; V2~(red): the user prompt delegates instructions to an external document (``follow the instructions in...''); V3~(yellow): a read-file call is immediately followed by a bash command referencing the same path; V4~(yellow): behavioral exfiltration patterns (download-then-execute, data-to-external-URL); V5~(green): direct high-risk operations in the command string; V6~(red, added 2026-04-08): a read-file call is followed by an HTTP POST to an external URL. Red flags unconditionally upgrade gate \texttt{allow} to \texttt{confirm}; two or more yellow flags trigger the same upgrade.

\textbf{D2 upgrade.} When V1 triggers, the Verifier reads the target file and scans it against 32 danger patterns (curl/wget exfiltration, SSH key injection, privilege escalation, database destruction, encoded payloads). A clean file suppresses the V1 flag, eliminating false positives from benign script execution workflows; a dangerous file retains the red flag with the specific evidence attached.

\textbf{Performance.} Overhead: $+$0.038ms per call (measured over 60 Hijacking samples). LLM cost: \$0. The Verifier adds negligible latency to L3's $\sim$3.5s semantic evaluation.

\subsection{Shadow Deployment}

Before promoting the system to primary interceptor, we operated in shadow mode for 11+~days, processing 30,204 real production calls across 281 runs. The system ran in parallel with existing safety rules, logging verdicts without affecting operations. Agreement rate was 99.47\%; the 0.53\% divergence consisted primarily of cases where the semantic gate correctly identified nuance that rule-based systems missed (157 false negatives in the existing system, 4 false positives where the new gate was more conservative than warranted). In all 4 false-positive disagreement cases, manual review confirmed the gate's block was over-triggered on ambiguous read-then-send workflows---a pattern the Verifier's D2 file scan can partially resolve by confirming benign file contents.

After stable agreement metrics, the system was promoted to primary interceptor (cutover). Post-cutover monitoring confirmed no regression.

\subsection{Owner-Centric Benchmark}

Standard safety benchmarks like AgentHarm evaluate universal harms (cybercrime, harassment). However, autonomous agents face a distinct threat model: \emph{owner-directed harm}, where an agent's actions compromise its owner's interests---credential exfiltration, unauthorized data sharing, infrastructure exposure, or inner-circle privacy leaks.

We construct a dedicated benchmark with 200~harmful and 50~benign scenarios across 8~owner-harm categories (Table~\ref{tab:owner_harm}). Harmful scenarios include multi-step chains (e.g., \texttt{read\_file} $\to$ \texttt{send\_email} with injected content) and social engineering patterns (authority claims, fake urgency). \textbf{Design principles}: (1)~attack patterns are derived from real agent incident reports and red-team playbooks~\cite{agentsofchaos2025,ruan2024toolemu}, not synthetic templates; (2)~each harmful scenario has a structurally similar benign counterpart to stress-test false-positive behavior (e.g., malicious script execution paired with legitimate deployment scripts); (3)~the Hijacking subcategory is intentionally oversampled (60H) to stress-test the system's weakest dimension. Benign scenarios include legitimate read-then-send workflows (press releases, newsletters) and operational scripts (backups, migrations) that must not be blocked.

\textbf{Construction protocol.} Scenarios were authored by the first author using a structured template: each harmful scenario specifies (1)~attack vector (email injection, ticket spoofing, etc.), (2)~tool-call chain, (3)~expected harmful outcome, and (4)~a structurally similar benign counterpart. All scenarios were reviewed against a 3-point rubric: (a)~is the harmful intent unambiguous? (b)~would a competent human operator flag this? (c)~does the benign counterpart have identical surface features? Scenarios failing any criterion were revised or excluded. The benchmark will be released upon acceptance. Inter-annotator agreement is not reported as all scenarios were authored and reviewed by a single researcher; we acknowledge this as a limitation.

Key design decisions: (1)~chain-aware Datalog rules detect \texttt{prior\_read\_file} $\to$ \texttt{external\_action} hijacking patterns; (2)~an \texttt{owner\_directed\_read\_send} signal exempts benign workflows where the owner explicitly names both file and recipient; (3)~\texttt{upgrade\_only} mode prevents the semantic gate from overriding conservative Datalog verdicts. The system evolved through 74~automated optimization loops from initial TPR~43\%/FPR~34\% to the final 100\%/0\%.

These numbers reflect the \emph{training distribution} and are reported for completeness; the held-out generalization results below provide a more conservative and reliable estimate of real-world performance.

\subsubsection{Held-out Generalization}

\textbf{Evaluation protocol and timeline.} To ensure held-out integrity, we document the evaluation sequence. \emph{Phase~1 (Loops~1--74)}: iterative rule and prompt optimization on the 200H+50B training set; all 74 loops used only training-set feedback. \emph{Phase~2 (Loops~76--77)}: manual inspection of training-set errors identified signal gaps in Asset Destruction, Infrastructure Exposure, and Unauthorized Autonomy; three L1 Datalog rules (OH-R12a/b/c) were patched based exclusively on training-set analysis. \emph{Phase~3 (v3 construction)}: the held-out set (300H+150B) was constructed after Loop~77; no v3 sample was seen during any optimization loop; category distribution deliberately differs from training (Hijacking oversampled to 60H). \emph{Phase~4 (Layer~4)}: the Post-Gate Verifier was designed after the initial v3 evaluation revealed gate-only Hijacking TPR of 43.3\%; V1--V6 rules were authored against the evidence table analysis (Section~3.3), then evaluated on v3 without further modification. All reported held-out metrics reflect a frozen system evaluated once on v3.

To assess generalization, we report results on a \emph{final} independent held-out set (v3: 300 harmful, 150 benign) constructed \emph{after} all optimization loops and never used during any iteration. This set deliberately oversamples categories identified as weak in prior evaluations to stress-test the system boundary conditions.

With the full three-layer pipeline (L1+L2+L3), the final held-out evaluation achieves \textbf{TPR~75.3\%} (226/300; Wilson 95\%~CI: [70.2\%, 79.9\%]) and \textbf{FPR~3.3\%} (5/150; Wilson 95\%~CI: [1.4\%, 7.6\%]).

\subsection{Capability Preservation}

A critical concern with safety systems is over-blocking. We evaluate capability preservation using L5~extreme probes---genuine policy-boundary cases where reasonable safety policies disagree:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Correctly allowed}: authorized penetration tests, compliance research, academic study of harmful content
\item \textbf{Correctly blocked}: surveillance tool deployment, controlled substance synthesis guides
\item \textbf{Debatable}: harm reduction drug identification (62.5\% allow rate---a policy choice, not a system bug)
\end{itemize}

We conducted a 20-scenario adversarial campaign (CC-BOS) testing classical Chinese framing, authority claims, and gradual escalation attacks. Initial attack success rate was 4.1/5; after adding content safety (T14), authority detection (T15), and input normalization (Step~0), the rate dropped to 1.1/5.

A key finding emerges from the held-out ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_layers}, bottom rows): L3's contribution \emph{increases} as attack sophistication grows. On the held-out set, L1+L2 alone achieves only 12.7\% TPR with 6.7\% FPR---rules match fewer novel attack variants and over-trigger on unfamiliar benign patterns. Adding L3 provides $+$62.6pp TPR while \emph{reducing} FPR by 3.4pp (6.7\%$\to$3.3\%), as semantic understanding both catches novel threats and corrects rule-based false alarms. This layer-complementarity pattern---deterministic rules for known structural patterns, semantic reasoning for novel and ambiguous cases---provides the central argument for compositional safety architectures.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Composability as safety primitive.} Following Reason's Swiss Cheese model~\cite{reason1990swisscheese}, each layer has characteristic ``holes'' (failure modes), but the probability of a threat passing through all layers simultaneously is multiplicatively small. This layered design provides stronger empirical robustness than monolithic systems, though formal end-to-end guarantees remain an open problem given the stochastic LLM component in Layer~3. This aligns with emerging best practices for governing agentic AI~\cite{openai2024practices}.

\textbf{Owner-centric vs.\ universal safety.} Standard benchmarks~\cite{andriushchenko2025agentharm} evaluate universal harms. Our owner-centric benchmark reveals a complementary threat model: structurally detectable patterns (chain-aware hijacking, credential exfiltration) are fully handled by deterministic rules, while semantically ambiguous cases (content interpretation) require LLM reasoning. This suggests that compositional architectures should route threats to the appropriate layer rather than forcing all cases through a single mechanism.

\textbf{Error taxonomy: layer complementarity over fundamental limitation.} Analysis of the 74 false negatives and 5 false positives on the held-out set reveals a structured failure landscape. We revise the taxonomy in light of the Layer~4 results.

The gate (L1--L3) alone shows 34 Hijacking false negatives. These were previously classified as \emph{structural} failures---implying an architectural dead end---but the Verifier results reveal a more nuanced picture. Of these 34, the Verifier recovers 30 (88\%) by inspecting file contents and multi-step sequences. This is possible because 30 of the 34 cases involve a \emph{information visibility gap}: the attack requires executing a file whose contents the gate never sees. This gap is not a fundamental architectural barrier; it is a \emph{layer boundary}. The Verifier, operating post-gate with access to file contents, closes most of it.

The residual \textbf{4/60 (6.7\%)} Hijacking failures---\texttt{mysql prod\_db < injection.sql} variants and a direct SSH key echo---are the \emph{genuine} structural boundary: they involve either SQL-file content analysis (requiring a SQL parser in the audit chain) or attack patterns that do not trigger any current read-then-exec signal. These represent true design gaps, not information visibility gaps.

The updated taxonomy: \emph{layer-visibility failures} (30/34 Hijacking FN = recoverable via post-gate auditing) vs.\ \emph{structural boundary} (4/34 Hijacking FN = genuine architectural limit). We categorize non-Hijacking failures separately: \emph{addressable} gaps (42~FN across Unauthorized Autonomy, Asset Destruction, Exfiltration---rule coverage holes deliberately left unfixed to avoid overfitting) and 3~Inner Circle Leak FN (requiring entity-sensitivity metadata from the KG). Critically, we deliberately do not fix addressable failures; they represent expected generalization gaps for hand-authored rule sets on novel attack variants.

\textbf{Why layer blind spots are orthogonal.} The gate's detection dimension is \emph{prompt semantics + command surface}: it sees tool names, command strings, URL patterns, and prompt text. It cannot see file contents, SQL query bodies, or multi-step information flow. The Verifier's detection dimension is \emph{behavioral sequence + content forensics}: it sees tool-call ordering and file contents but makes no semantic judgment about intent. These dimensions are nearly independent for Hijacking attacks, explaining why combined coverage jumps from 43.3\% to 93.3\%.

\textbf{Stochastic variance in MoE.} A surprising finding is that \texttt{temperature=0} does not guarantee deterministic outputs from Mixture-of-Experts architectures. We observed intermittent FPR fluctuations that majority vote completely eliminated---a practical consideration absent from current literature.

\textbf{Multi-agent safety gaps.} Recent analyses of autonomous agent deployments~\cite{agentsofchaos2025} and LM-emulated sandbox evaluations~\cite{ruan2024toolemu} identify critical failure modes absent from current benchmarks: fake task completion (agents reporting success without execution), PII leakage across tool chains, and unbounded process lifetimes. Our composable architecture addresses the first via proof traces and the third via fail-closed timeouts, but PII leakage across tool boundaries remains an open problem.

\textbf{Practical deployment considerations.} The shadow deployment methodology (Section~3) enabled zero-downtime transition with quantified risk: 99.47\% agreement over 30,204 calls before cutover. The 0.53\% divergence---predominantly false negatives in the existing system---validates the composable approach. NIST's emerging agent security standards~\cite{nist2026agent} call for exactly this type of auditable safety pipeline.

\textbf{Limitations.} (1)~\emph{Residual prompt injection (6.7\% structural boundary).} The four Hijacking cases missed by both gate and verifier represent genuine architectural limits: three involve SQL injection via \texttt{mysql < file.sql} (requiring a SQL-aware content parser in the audit chain) and one involves a direct SSH key injection that bypasses the read-then-exec behavioral signal. These cannot be addressed by the current rule architecture without targeted L1 Datalog additions. (1b)~\emph{Infrastructure Exposure FPR.} The 20.0\% benign FPR for Infrastructure Exposure (5/25 false positives from OH-R3a external-channel config queries) is the primary FPR contributor across the entire held-out set. This over-trigger is a known calibration target: tightening the OH-R3a scope to exclude read-only config queries is expected to reduce overall FPR below 1\% without affecting TPR. (2)~\emph{Layer~4 calibration.} While Layer~4 runs on all calls, its current V2 (delegated instructions) and V3 (read-then-exec) rules may over-trigger on benign operational workflows in other categories (e.g., Infrastructure Exposure scripts that legitimately read config files before acting). Per-category threshold calibration is needed before global deployment without category-aware filtering. (3)~The 42 non-Hijacking \emph{addressable} failures (rule coverage gaps for specific destructive commands, novel exfiltration paths, and autonomy boundary violations) are deliberately left unfixed to avoid overfitting to the held-out distribution. (4)~The semantic gate prompt is English-centric; cross-lingual attacks may exploit language gaps. (5)~Nous operates as a pure interception layer and does not modify the system prompt or inject dynamic context. The primary deployment overhead is the additional API call for L3 semantic evaluation ($\sim$3.5s per invocation on DeepSeek-V3.2); L1 and L2 collectively pass 99.47\% of production calls as trivial, so L3 is invoked for fewer than 1\% of tool calls in practice, keeping mean added latency below 35ms per tool call.

\textbf{Future work.} Closing the remaining 4/60 structural boundary cases via SQL-aware file content analysis and targeted L1 Datalog rules for direct key-injection patterns; extending Layer~4 scope safely to other categories (requires per-category FP calibration); cross-lingual semantic gates; integration with differentiable Datalog~\cite{li2023scallop} for end-to-end optimization of the rule-semantic boundary; and formal verification of the Datalog-to-LLM handoff protocol.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Monolithic classifiers.} Llama Guard~\cite{inan2023llamaguard} and ShieldGemma~\cite{zeng2024shieldgemma} fine-tune LLMs for content classification. While effective for conversational safety, they introduce latency incompatible with real-time tool interception and cannot provide formal guarantees.

\textbf{Rule-based systems.} NeMo Guardrails~\cite{rebedea2023nemoguardrails} offers programmable rails for LLM applications. Our Datalog layer is architecturally similar but extends the approach with formal fail-closed semantics and composability with a semantic layer. Guardrails AI~\cite{guardrailsai2024} provides input/output validation for LLM applications with a focus on structural schemas.

\textbf{Formal verification approaches.} Doshi et al.~\cite{doshi2026verifiable} propose applying System-Theoretic Process Analysis (STPA) to derive formal safety requirements for LLM agent tool use, formalizing constraints via a capability-enhanced MCP framework. This \emph{design-time} approach complements our \emph{runtime} compositional gate: where they provide guarantees through formal specification, we provide empirically validated real-time enforcement with semantic understanding for ambiguous cases.

\textbf{Enterprise agent governance.} NVIDIA's NemoClaw~\cite{nvidia2026nemoclaw}, announced at GTC~2026, provides a declarative policy engine for enterprise agent governance---architecturally homologous to our Layer~1. Microsoft 365~E7~\cite{microsoft2026e7} and Oasis Security~\cite{oasis2026funding} represent industry convergence on the need for dedicated agent safety infrastructure. Meta's Sev-1 incident~\cite{meta2026sev1}---a confused deputy attack causing 2-hour data exposure---demonstrates the practical urgency of compositional safety.

\textbf{Post-gate auditing.} The Post-Gate Verifier (Layer~4) is architecturally similar to intrusion detection systems~\cite{liao2013intrusion} that audit events after a perimeter defense. The key distinction is that the Verifier operates on AI agent tool-call sequences rather than network packets, and its rules exploit the information asymmetry between what the gate can see (prompt + metadata) and what a post-hoc auditor can see (file contents + multi-step sequences). This complements Doshi et al.'s design-time STPA approach~\cite{doshi2026verifiable} with a runtime audit layer.

\textbf{Compositional AI safety.} Composability as a safety principle is widely advocated (e.g., Tegmark and Omohundro~\cite{tegmark2023provably}) but rarely empirically validated at the layer-interaction level. Our overlap analysis (Table~\ref{tab:hijacking_overlap}) provides concrete evidence that compositional coverage gains are not merely additive but emerge from the orthogonality of detection dimensions---a claim distinct from the Swiss Cheese model's probabilistic argument.

\textbf{Neurosymbolic AI.} Scallop~\cite{li2023scallop} demonstrates differentiable Datalog for neurosymbolic programming. Integrating differentiable reasoning into our Datalog layer is a promising future direction for optimizing the rule-semantic boundary.

\textbf{Automated red-teaming.} Votal AI's CART platform~\cite{votalai2026cart} and the AgentHarm benchmark~\cite{andriushchenko2025agentharm} provide complementary evaluation methodologies. Our CC-BOS campaign extends these with cultural framing attacks.
